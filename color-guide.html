Color Guide

How colors are compared

Three ideas drive every match in this tool: ΔE (how different two colors are), undertone (which direction a color leans), and depth (how light or dark it is). Together they describe a shade more precisely than any single name.

What is ΔE?

ΔE (Delta E) is a single number that tells you how different two colors are — as perceived by the human eye. The smaller the number, the more similar the colors. It's the industry standard for measuring color accuracy in cosmetics, printing, and design.

ΔE = √(ΔL*² + Δa*² + Δb*²)

Colors are measured in CIELAB space — a 3D model designed to match human vision. L* is lightness, a* is red–green, and b* is yellow–blue. ΔE is simply the straight-line distance between two points in that space.

How we use ΔE in this tool

Each lipstick in our database has been measured in CIELAB color space using a spectrophotometer. When you select a color on the wheel, we compute the ΔE distance from that color to every product in the database and surface the closest matches — ranked from most to least similar.

A ΔE below 5 typically means the product is a very strong candidate for that consumer's extracted shade. A ΔE between 5 and 10 is still useful as a reference — same color family, slightly different depth or saturation. Above 15, the match is more of an inspiration than a substitute.

Tip for retail advisors: always show the consumer the actual swatch alongside the ΔE number. The number gives scientific confidence; the swatch gives the emotional "yes, that's my color" moment.

ΔE < 3 — exact dupe ΔE 3–6 — same vibe, slightly different ΔE 6–12 — same family ΔE > 12 — new direction

Undertone — how a color leans

Undertone describes which direction a color leans, independent of how light or dark it is. A color can be cool, neutral, or warm — and you can find all three within the same family. There are cool reds and warm reds, cool pinks and warm pinks.

Cool red
Leans toward blue or pink. Berries, fuchsias, blue‑based reds.
Neutral red
Balanced — neither blue‑ nor orange‑leaning. True reds and balanced rose.
Warm red
Leans toward orange or yellow. Bricks, corals, terracotta reds.

Technically we compute the hue angle in CIELAB space — the direction the color points on the a*–b* plane. Hues under ~12° are cool, 12–28° neutral, above 28° warm.

Depth — how light or dark

Depth describes how light or dark a shade appears — separate from undertone or finish. A light shade can be sheer or pigmented; a deep shade can be matte or glossy. Depth only refers to the color itself.

Light
Soft and quiet on the lip. Pales, sheers, washes of color.
Medium
Pigmented but not heavy. The everyday range — most reds and pinks live here.
Deep
Bold and saturated. Vampy, plum, oxblood, deep berry.

Technically we read L* (lightness) from CIELAB: under 32 is deep, 32–55 is medium, above 55 is light.
